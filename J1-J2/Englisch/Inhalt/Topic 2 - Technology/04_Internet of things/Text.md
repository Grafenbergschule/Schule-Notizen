
### **Tracking of People in Their Smart Homes**

- Smart homes function as a "nosy roommate" that monitors personal habits and daily routines.
    
- Devices track what residents watch on TV, the specific times lights are turned on or off in different rooms, and when someone enters or leaves the house using a key fob.
    
- The "Internet of Things" (IoT) captures a continuous stream of data from common appliances like thermostats and fridges, which could potentially be sold.
    
- Surveillance ties have been documented between home security systems, such as Amazon's Ring, and law enforcement agencies.
    

### **Use of Utility Records**

- Law enforcement and government agencies, specifically U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), have used utility records to pursue immigration violations.
    
- These records include data on phone, water, and electricity usage.
    
- Authorities use this "playbook" to buy up hundreds of millions of records to track and identify individuals.
    

### **Privacy Protection in the USA**

- Privacy protections in the United States are described as "generally quite weak".
    
- The legal system is attempting to address modern 21st-century digital threats with 18th-century rights established at the country's founding.
    
- While physical papers and objects in a home are protected, digital data stored in the cloud often falls into a legal "gray zone" with far fewer protections.
    
- There is a lack of strong federal privacy protections regarding the collection and sale of data from smart devices.
    

### **Third-Party Data Collection**

- Under the "third-party doctrine," data held by private companies is generally open to police access without a warrant.
    
- A massive "data brokerage" economy exists where companies profit by buying and selling the data generated by smart-home systems.
    
- The government often bypasses direct surveillance by purchasing this data from private firms that have already collected it from consumers.
